Doubts are emerging regarding Spain's commitment to renewable energy. In Aragon, north-eastern Spain, a massive wind turbine in the town of Figueruelas spins as a symbol of the region's green energy importance. This status is being reinforced by the construction of a nearby factory that will produce electric vehicle batteries. The project involves a combined €4bn investment from Chinese firm CATL and Netherlands-based Stellantis. Yao Jing, the Chinese ambassador, described it as one of the largest Chinese investments in European history.

Mayor Luis Bertol Moreno states the area was a logical choice due to its year-round wind and abundant sunshine. He notes that the surrounding wind turbines and solar panels will be crucial for powering the new factory. The plant is seen as a vindication of Spain's energy model, which prioritizes renewables. Renewable sources rose from contributing one-third of Spain's electricity in 2017 to 57% last year, with a goal of 81% by 2030. Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez recently championed this approach with the slogan "Green, baby, green".

However, this commitment has faced scrutiny following an April 28 blackout that left parts of Spain and Portugal in the dark. Opposition leader Alberto Núñez Feijóo accused the government of "fanaticism" and suggested an over-reliance on renewables caused the outage. Critics point to solar generation hitting a record 61.5% just a week prior. Yet, the government and grid operator Red Eléctrica deny this link, attributing the blackout to an "unknown event" and voltage oscillations rather than the renewable rate. Cyber-attacks have been ruled out.

Meanwhile, Spain's nuclear industry, which provides 20% of the electricity, is pushing back against plans to close five plants by 2035. While other nations revive nuclear power, Spain remains an outlier. Ignacio Araluce of Foro Nuclear argues that nuclear energy provides stability and complements renewables, which cannot operate around the clock. Opposition parties, including Vox, support nuclear power as a "crucial source of stability". Ms Sánchez acknowledges the need for better storage and grid connections. Political instability further complicates the future, with potential elections looming that could shift focus back to traditional energy. For Figueruelas, however, the transition promises growth, with the new battery plant expected to create thousands of jobs and revitalize the area
